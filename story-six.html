About

Science and art are a powerful combination for expanding perspectives in healthcare. Dr. Heleen Eising, gynaecologist and researcher, has gathered personal stories from individuals across Europe living with a hereditary bleeding disorder. These narratives not only challenge conventional approaches to women's healthcare but also aim to inspire healthcare professionals to rethink how they engage with patients and approach care. This project reflects how patient stories, art (graphic design, literature, music), science and care (gynaecology, medical humanities) can bring a fresh perspective to women's healthcare. As Heleen would say: Theatre in healthcare for new ways of seeing.

Junction explores how imagination and dialogue can drive improvements in healthcare through workshops, talks, conversations, scientific research and tools. Read more

Read the paper

This website features stories from women with blood clotting disorders, aimed at sharing experiences and improving care, with involvement from family members, scientists, and (future) healthcare professionals. The stories are preserved for ten years to ensure they remain useful in the future. The stories on this site were collected after women responded to a call on LinkedIn. The participants all have different clotting disorders, varying in severity and symptoms. The stories presented here are summaries of the original accounts. All participants have given permission for their stories to be shared anonymously and used for scientific research. For scientific collaboration, please contact us.

Break the Cycle of Silence

Story
Six

“
Break the Cycle of Silence and Create Awareness.

She was only nine when she got her first period - years ahead of most of her classmates.

She was only nine when she got her first period, years ahead of most of her classmates. “I was very sick from it,” she recalls. “I leaked through, of course I was ashamed of that too.” At the time, no one considered a bleeding disorder. Not even when she repeatedly visited hospitals for severe nosebleeds. It wasn’t until her twenties that she was finally diagnosed with von Willebrand Disease (VWD).

fig. 01 fig. 02
fig. 03

By then, she had endured years of unexplained symptoms such as heavy menstruation, frequent bruising, gum bleeding, and complications after surgery. “I had surgery twice before the diagnosis. My arm was so purple under the cast that people said it could not be normal. Yet no one considered a bleeding disorder.” The turning point came when a pulmonologist finally noticed her bruises. “Someone had to be alert,” she says. “That is how I found out. Someone asked the right questions.” This feeling of truly being seen recurs throughout her story. Her relationship with the condition is complicated. “I do everything. I even get tattoos,” she laughs. “But I also live with uncertainty.” She suspects that hormonal changes affect her symptoms, especially bruising and bleeding. “You start to wonder how much of this is related to my hormonal cycle. Why does no one connect the dots?”

“I do everything. I even get tattoos,” she laughs. “But I also live with uncertainty.”
fig. 04

Now a mother, she watches her son face the same condition. His symptoms are different and more severe, but he receives consistent care. Every three months, she checks in with his hematologist. For herself, she admits, “Sometimes I miss it. I don’t go for regular check-ups.”Today, she prepares carefully for every medical appointment. “I write down my questions in advance because otherwise, you forget when you are there.” Over time, she has become more assertive, even when it is difficult. “I recently told a doctor that I had lost some trust. He took it well, but it took me years to learn how to speak up.” She feels that communication between healthcare professionals still needs to improve. “They should work together as a team. A doctor or nurse at school should notice if a young girl frequently has nosebleeds and later experiences heavy periods.”

“
As a parent, she tries to break that cycle of silence and wishes to create awareness for bleeding disorders. “They said my son was cautious on the playground. I said, yes, that’s because of us. We’re always warning him - ‘watch out.’ I feel guilty. But at the same time, I want him to live freely.”

Medical background

Von Willebrand Explained

Von Willebrand disease (VWD) is an inherited bleeding disorder that affects the blood's ability to clot properly. People with VWD either have a deficiency of, or a dysfunction in, the von Willebrand factor (VWF), which is crucial for forming a platelet plug during the clotting process. As a result, they may not be able to form this platelet plug effectively, or it may take longer to form. This disorder is quite common, affecting approximately 1 in every 100 people. It occurs equally in men and women, but women may experience more symptoms due to heavy menstrual bleeding.
