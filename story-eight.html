About

Science and art are a powerful combination for expanding perspectives in healthcare. Dr. Heleen Eising, gynaecologist and researcher, has gathered personal stories from individuals across Europe living with a hereditary bleeding disorder. These narratives not only challenge conventional approaches to women's healthcare but also aim to inspire healthcare professionals to rethink how they engage with patients and approach care. This project reflects how patient stories, art (graphic design, literature, music), science and care (gynaecology, medical humanities) can bring a fresh perspective to women's healthcare. As Heleen would say: Theatre in healthcare for new ways of seeing.

Junction explores how imagination and dialogue can drive improvements in healthcare through workshops, talks, conversations, scientific research and tools. Read more

Read the paper

This website features stories from women with blood clotting disorders, aimed at sharing experiences and improving care, with involvement from family members, scientists, and (future) healthcare professionals. The stories are preserved for ten years to ensure they remain useful in the future. The stories on this site were collected after women responded to a call on LinkedIn. The participants all have different clotting disorders, varying in severity and symptoms. The stories presented here are summaries of the original accounts. All participants have given permission for their stories to be shared anonymously and used for scientific research. For scientific collaboration, please contact us.

Take the Jump to Move Forward

Sometimes, You Have to Take the Jump to Move Forward.

Diagnosed with severe Factor VII deficiency as an infant, she has spent her life learning to navigate a world where few understand what her condition entails. “Sometimes, you have to take a leap to move forward”, she says. I was able to make that leap because I had support even if it was not perfect.” A major learning curve came with understanding her menstrual health. “I did not realise my period was too heavy until I learned about the 7-2-1 rule in my thirties,” she explains. With improved self-monitoring, daily injections during menstruation, and iron treatments that finally worked, she says she now feels like “a new person.”

Becoming a mother as a severe Factor VII deficiency patient was a giant leap to take. Her hematologist first raised the topic at a routine check-up when she turned 30 - coincidentally just days after she met her now-partner. “It really, really stressed me out,” she recalls, “I’m glad we took the time we needed to decide if we wanted children.” Because of scientific limited knowledge of pregnancies in severe Factor VII deficiency women, a pregnancy would be high-risk and entail enormous challenges. She took the risk.

Together with a team of healthcare professionals, she prepared as thoroughly as possible, carefully testing how quickly her Factor VII medication wore off to adjust her treatment during pregnancy. However, nothing can fully prepare you for childbirth, and difficult decisions still had to be made at critical moments. “During labour, they asked if they could use a vacuum because the baby’s heartbeat was concerning. By then, an emergency C-section was no longer an option,” she explains. “We had not tested my partner’s Factor VII levels during my pregnancy. We estimated the baby’s Factor VII level and accepted the calculated risk of bleeding.”

fig. 04

The lack of medical knowledge became critical when she arrived at the hospital in labour. Although there was a birth plan, the hematologist on duty hesitated about the correct dosages during her labour, delaying necessary treatment for more than half an hour. “I had brought three doses of Factor medication with me,” she recalls, “but they waited for a fourth dose to arrive instead of using the three I already had. Being too conservative, cautious can actually increase the risk.”

In spite of the stressful situation, she found moments of meaninful support - like the midwife who stayed with her from start to finish, or the doctors who debriefed the birth with their team and came back to share what they’d learned to support other women with rare bleeding disorders.Yet amidst the stressful situation, she found moments of real support - like the midwife who stayed with her from start to finish, or the doctors who debriefed the birth with their team and came back to share what they’d learned to support other women with rare bleeding disorders.

She’s seen how her condition shapes other choices in her life as well. She avoids physically demanding jobs, has learned to advocate for herself at work, and has gradually become more open about her condition. Still, she says, “I'm more than a bleeding disorder.”

Her message is clear: listen to patients, ask specific questions, and never assume everything is normal. “Talking is crucial,” she says. “Be curious, ask, and hear your patient’s story. I know my condition is rare. But I still deserve to be understood.”

Medical background

Factor VII (Factor 7) deficiency

Factor VII (Factor 7) deficiency is a rare inherited bleeding disorder, affecting approximately 1 in 500,000 people worldwide. It is an autosomal recessive condition, meaning that both parents must carry and pass on the genetic mutation for a child to be affected.

Factor VII is one of several proteins in the blood that help form clots to stop bleeding. When this protein is missing or not working properly, it can lead to excessive or spontaneous bleeding, such as heavy menstrual bleeding, joint bleeds, hematomas, or anemia. Bleeding episodes can become especially dangerous during surgery, childbirth, or after trauma.

Treatment options vary depending on the situation and severity of the deficiency. They include recombinant factor VIIa (such as NovoSeven®), which is a lab-made version of the protein, as well as plasma-derived factor VII concentrates from donated blood. In some cases, prothrombin complex concentrates (PCC), which contain several clotting factors, or fresh frozen plasma (FFP) may be used to manage bleeding.
